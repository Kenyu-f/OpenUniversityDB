## 2. History

Caltech originated as a small vocational and preparatory school, **Throop University**, founded in Pasadena in 1891 by local businessman and politician Amos G. Throop. The school was subsequently renamed Throop Polytechnic Institute, and later Throop College of Technology, before adopting its present name, the **California Institute of Technology**, in 1920.

The institution's transformation into a research-focused institute of science and engineering began in the early 20th century under the leadership of astronomer **George Ellery Hale**, who joined the Board of Trustees in 1907 and recruited prominent scientists including chemist **Arthur Amos Noyes** and physicist **Robert Andrews Millikan**. The vocational and preparatory divisions were phased out and spun off (the preparatory program became an independent Polytechnic School in 1907; vocational training was discontinued by 1910).

Key historical milestones:

- **1917** — The Norman Bridge Laboratory of Physics is established, drawing Robert Millikan from the University of Chicago.
- **1920** — Institution formally renamed the California Institute of Technology.
- **1921–1945** — Robert Millikan serves as "Chairman of the Executive Council," Caltech's de facto president; the period is sometimes called "Millikan's School."
- **1926–1928** — Formal academic divisions begin to coalesce, including the Guggenheim Graduate School of Aeronautics (1926) and the Division of Biology (1928, under Thomas Hunt Morgan).
- **1931–1933** — Albert Einstein makes multiple extended visits to Caltech as a visiting scholar.
- **1934** — Caltech is elected to the Association of American Universities (AAU).
- **1936–1943** — Graduate students working under Theodore von Kármán conduct early rocketry experiments, which evolve into the **Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL)**.
- **1946–1969** — Under president Lee A. DuBridge, Caltech's faculty and campus roughly double/triple in size; new fields such as chemical biology, planetary science, and geochemistry emerge.
- **1948** — Dedication of the 200-inch Hale Telescope at Palomar Observatory.
- **1958** — JPL, operated by Caltech, launches Explorer 1, the first U.S. satellite; JPL becomes NASA's first federally funded research and development center.
- **2016** — LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory), co-founded by Caltech and MIT, achieves the first direct observation of gravitational waves.
- **2026** — Ray Jayawardhana begins service as Caltech's 10th president, succeeding Thomas F. Rosenbaum.

## 3. Governance and Organization

Caltech is incorporated as a non-profit corporation, governed by a privately appointed **46-member Board of Trustees** who serve five-year terms and retire at age 72. The Board elects a **president** (chief executive officer), who is supported by a **provost** (chief academic officer) and additional vice-presidential and senior leadership positions.

- **President (as of July 2026):** Ray Jayawardhana, also holder of the Sonja and William Davidow Presidential Chair and Professor of Astronomy.
- **Chair of the Faculty (as of July 2026):** Azita Emami, Andrew and Peggy Cherng Professor of Electrical Engineering and Medical Engineering.

Caltech also manages the **Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL)** for NASA under contract, and oversees major shared research facilities including LIGO, the W. M. Keck Observatory (co-managed with the University of California), and the Palomar Observatory.

## 5. Research Strengths

Caltech is classified by the Carnegie Classification as an **R1: Doctoral University – Very High Research Activity** institution. Areas of particular research strength, based on institutional history, affiliated Nobel Prizes, and major managed facilities, include:

- Physics (quantum and particle physics, condensed matter, gravitational-wave astrophysics via LIGO)
- Astronomy and astrophysics (Palomar, Keck, and space-telescope-affiliated research)
- Chemistry and chemical engineering (physical chemistry, chemical biology)
- Planetary science and geology (seismology, planetary exploration via JPL)
- Biology and biological engineering (genetics, molecular biology, neuroscience)
- Aerospace and mechanical engineering
- Computer science and computation and mathematical sciences
- Environmental science and sustainability

## 7. Notable Achievements

- **Nobel Prizes:** As of recent counts, approximately 80 Nobel laureates have been affiliated with Caltech as alumni or faculty (varying methodologies count "affiliated" vs. "faculty-only" differently — see `alumni.md`). Chemist Linus Pauling is the only individual in history to win two unshared Nobel Prizes (Chemistry, 1954; Peace, 1962), and did so as a Caltech alumnus and long-time faculty member.
- **Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL):** Caltech has managed JPL for NASA since 1958; JPL traces its origins to rocketry experiments beginning in 1936.
- **LIGO:** Co-founded with MIT; achieved the first direct detection of gravitational waves in 2016, resulting in the 2017 Nobel Prize in Physics for LIGO's founders.
- **Palomar and Keck Observatories:** Caltech operates and co-manages major optical astronomical facilities, including the historic 200-inch Hale Telescope.
- **Seismology:** The Caltech-Carnegie Seismological Laboratory (est. 1927) was the site of the development of the Richter magnitude scale by Charles Richter and Beno Gutenberg in 1932.

## 9. Collaborations and Managed Facilities

- **NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL)** — federally funded research and development center managed by Caltech for NASA.
- **LIGO** — gravitational-wave observatory, joint with MIT.
- **W. M. Keck Observatory** — co-managed with the University of California.
- **Palomar Observatory** — owned and operated by Caltech.
- **Owens Valley Radio Observatory and Big Bear Solar Observatory** — Caltech-affiliated astronomical facilities.
- **Joint MD/PhD programs** with the Keck School of Medicine of USC, the UCLA David Geffen School of Medicine, and the Kaiser Permanente Bernard J. Tyson School of Medicine.
