# Caltech — Roadmap for Aspiring Researchers

`universities/caltech/roadmap.md`

> A roadmap for a high school student whose goal is to conduct research at Caltech, synthesized from Caltech's stated admissions priorities (academic rigor in math/physics/chemistry, demonstrated research interest) and the structure of its undergraduate research pipeline (SURF, WAVE). This is **guidance, not an official Caltech document** — cross-reference with `admissions.md` and `undergraduate_research.md` for the underlying sourced requirements.

---

## 1. High School Preparation (Foundational)

- Caltech's academic requirements are unusually specific for a general university: admissions explicitly requires demonstrated preparation in **calculus, chemistry, and physics** — not just "strong grades," but specific coursework or equivalent certification (see `admissions.md`).
- Take the most rigorous math/science track your school offers. If your school lacks AP/IB-level Calculus BC, Chemistry, or Physics 1, Caltech accepts certification via **Schoolhouse.world** course challenges as a substitute — start this early if your school's offerings are limited.
- Build strong **writing skills** early — Caltech's application essays and (later) SURF research proposals both demand clear technical writing, not just technical ability.

## 2. Mathematics Track

- Progress through **calculus (single- and multivariable), linear algebra, and differential equations** before or during senior year if possible — these are foundational for nearly every Caltech research area, from physics to biology.
- Consider proof-based math (introductory real analysis, discrete math, or a "bridge to higher math" course) if available — Caltech's own math curriculum is proof-heavy from an early stage (see `laboratories/mathematics.md`).
- Participate in math competitions (see Section 6) — these build the problem-solving stamina Caltech's curriculum expects from day one.

## 3. Science/Domain Track

- **Physics:** Complete a full mechanics + electricity/magnetism sequence (AP Physics C or equivalent) if targeting physics/engineering/astronomy research.
- **Chemistry:** Complete a rigorous first-year chemistry course (AP Chemistry or equivalent) — required academic preparation per Caltech admissions.
- If your interests lean toward biology, geology, or planetary science, don't neglect physics/chemistry/math fundamentals — Caltech's interdisciplinary culture means even biology research (see `graduate_research.md`) leans heavily on quantitative methods.

## 4. Programming and Computational Skills

- Learn at least one general-purpose programming language well (Python is the most common starting point in physical-science research contexts) before starting any research position — nearly all SURF/WAVE projects, even in "wet lab" fields, involve some data analysis or computational component.
- Basic familiarity with version control (Git) and command-line tools will smooth the transition into any university research group.
- If your interests lean computational/mathematical (e.g., Applied and Computational Mathematics, see `laboratories/mathematics.md`), begin exploring numerical methods and scientific computing libraries (NumPy, SciPy, etc.) in high school.

## 5. Early Research Experience

- **Before college:** Seek out any accessible research experience — a local university lab, a science fair project with genuine original work, an online research mentorship program, or a structured summer research program for high schoolers (e.g., RSI, SSP, or university-run high-school programs — evaluate these independently; they are not Caltech-run).
- **As a Caltech undergraduate:** Target **SURF** (Summer Undergraduate Research Fellowships) as early as your first or second summer — this is Caltech's flagship structured pathway into a faculty-mentored research project (see `undergraduate_research.md` for full mechanics, GPA eligibility, and the proposal-writing process).
- If you're from a background historically underrepresented in STEM PhD programs, investigate **WAVE Fellows** specifically — it's designed to build a pathway toward doctoral research (GPA threshold 3.4/4.0, sophomore standing or above).
- Treat every research experience as **proposal-driven**, mirroring Caltech's own SURF model: identify a question, write a short proposal, seek a mentor's buy-in — this habit transfers directly into how Caltech expects students to operate.

## 6. Competitions

- **Mathematics:** AMC/AIME → USAMO pathway (or national equivalent outside the U.S.); math olympiad participation signals both ability and stamina for proof-based reasoning.
- **Physics:** USAPhO (or national equivalent) / International Physics Olympiad pathway.
- **Chemistry:** USNCO (U.S. National Chemistry Olympiad) or equivalent.
- **General science/engineering:** Regeneron Science Talent Search, ISEF (International Science and Engineering Fair), or equivalent regional/national science fairs — these reward the same "original research + written proposal + oral defense" pattern Caltech itself uses for SURF.
- **Competitions are a signal, not a requirement** — Caltech's admissions process is holistic (see `admissions.md`); deep, sustained engagement in one or two areas is generally viewed more favorably than shallow participation in many.

## 7. Reading List (Illustrative, Not Official)

*(These are widely recommended, field-agnostic starting points for a student aiming at Caltech-style research culture — not a Caltech-endorsed list.)*

- **"Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!"** — Richard Feynman (Caltech professor) — a candid, non-technical window into Caltech's scientific culture.
- **"What Do You Care What Other People Think?"** — Richard Feynman — companion memoir, includes his account of the Challenger disaster investigation.
- **The Feynman Lectures on Physics** (available freely via Caltech, see `resources.md`) — foundational physics text originally developed for Caltech undergraduates.
- Any accessible popular-science book in your specific field of interest (astrophysics, molecular biology, planetary science, etc.) to test genuine curiosity before committing years of preparation.

## 8. University (Undergraduate) Application Preparation

- Confirm current-cycle **standardized testing policy** (SAT/ACT requirement has changed multiple times in recent years — verify against `admissions.md` and the official admissions site before applying).
- Prepare **teacher recommendations** from math/science teachers specifically, per Caltech's supplement guidance.
- Draft application essays that demonstrate **genuine intellectual curiosity and initiative** (e.g., independent projects, competition results, research experience) rather than generic achievement narratives — Caltech's small, research-intensive culture selects heavily for self-directed technical curiosity.
- International applicants: confirm English-proficiency exam requirements (DET/IELTS/TOEFL) well before the November 1 (REA) or January 5 (RD) deadlines.

## 9. Graduate School Preparation (For Those Continuing to a Caltech PhD)

- Build a **strong, specific research record** as an undergraduate — Caltech PhD admissions weighs research fit with individual faculty members heavily (see `graduate_research.md`, Advisor Selection).
- Cultivate relationships with faculty mentors who can write **substantive, specific letters of recommendation** — generic letters are a known weak point in competitive PhD applications.
- Write a **Statement of Purpose** that clearly identifies specific Caltech faculty whose research aligns with your interests, not just "Caltech is prestigious" — reflecting the same fit-driven culture as SURF/WAVE.
- If GRE is required/recommended by your target option, confirm current policy directly with that option — it varies significantly across Caltech departments (see `admissions.md`, Graduate Admissions).
- Consider applying to **SURF or WAVE Fellows at Caltech as an undergraduate from another institution** — both accept non-Caltech students and are a well-documented way to build a relationship with a potential future PhD advisor before formally applying.
